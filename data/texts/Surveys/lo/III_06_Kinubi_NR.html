Kinubi

Xavier Luffin

Kinubi

Autoglossonyms:

Kinbi, Rutn Nbi

Number of speakers:

unknown

Major lexifier:

Sudanese Arabic

Other contributing languages:

Egyptian Arabic; Swahili, English (contemporary prestige languages)

Location:

Uganda and Kenya; communities in DR Congo and South Sudan; formerly spoken in Somalia and Tanzania

Official languages of Kenya and Uganda:

English and Swahili

 

1.    Introduction

Kinubi (autoglossonyms: Kinbi or Rutn nbi) is spoken today mainly in Uganda and in Kenya. It is spoken as a first language by the Nubi. According to some sources, it is also spoken as a lingua franca by non-Nubi in some towns of western Uganda. Most of the Nubi also speak Swahili and English. Formerly, there were some Nubi communities in Tanzania and Somalia too, but it seems that the Tanzanian Nubi do not speak the language anymore — it has been replaced by Swahili; the Nubi of Somalia accompanied the British when they left a part of Somalia to the Italians in 1921, and they then settled in Kenya. Some Nubi communities also live in the Democratic Republic of Congo, mainly in the cities close to the Ugandan border.

     Kinubi is closely related to Juba Arabic (see Manfredi & Petrollino (2012) in this volume), but it also includes many words borrowed from Swahili and English. There are two main varieties of Kinubi, Ugandan Kinubi and Kenyan Kinubi, though they are very similar to each other.

2.    Sociohistorical background

The establishment of Egyptian trading and military camps in southern Sudan, in the second half of the 19th century, led to the use of an Arabic-based lingua franca as the means of communication between Arabic speakers (using several dialects, such as Egyptian and West Sudanese) and the multilingual local population, especially the local men who were introduced into the army. Slowly, this lingua franca became a pidgin in use among the soldiers and their families. At the end of the 19th century, some of these soldiers went to East Africa — Uganda, and later Kenya, Tanzania, and even Somalia — where they settled down, mainly serving in the British colonial troops (the Kings African Rifles). With them they brought their language, which would later be called Kinubi. Most Nubi still remember the names of their ancestors tribes (kabla in Kinubi) like Moru, Podjulu, Kakwa, Bari, Dinka, and Shilluk.

     These pidginized and creolized varieties of Arabic have been described by European observers since the beginning of the 20th century. A British officer, E. V. Jenkins, wrote a booklet in 1908–1909 about the pidginized Arabic spoken by Sudanese soldiers serving in Uganda (Kaye & Tosco 1993). In 1913, J. A. Meldon, another British officer, also wrote a lexicon of this language (Meldon 1913).

3.    Sociolinguistic situation

In East Africa, the Nubi communities are proud of their language and see it as one of the features of their identity. However, competition with English and Swahili may endanger the use of Kinubi, especially in the big cities like Mombasa or Nairobi, as it did in Tanzania, where it seems that Swahili has replaced Kinubi among the Nubi communities.

     Kinubi may be written in Latin characters: Some Nubi exchange letters and e-mails in their language or use it in the written administration of their cultural organizations, as in Mombasa, for instance. In Bombo (Uganda), Mustafer Khamisy writes poems in Kinubi and distributes them during Nubi cultural events.

     There is phonemic variation for some consonants, mainly s ~ š, j ~ g, l ~ r, and z ~ j, as in: azl ~ ajl man, snu ~ šnu what?.

    

     There is word stress, which may be lexically distinctive as in sba seven vs. sab morning, and even grammatically distinctive as in lmu to gather vs. lim to be gathered. In most cases, word stress in Kinubi corresponds to a long vowel or a stressed vowel in the lexifier, e.g. kalm word, azl man.

     The main patterns of the syllable are: V, VC, CV, and CVC, as in -na I, al (relative pronoun), fi in and kan if, but there is a strong tendency toward open syllabic structure, for instance with the use of an additional final vowel, as in kebr > kebri big.

The independent pronouns are used in four cases:

(i) subject of nominal sentences: mun Mundre [3PL Mundare] They are Mundare.

(ii) subject of verbal sentences: na rifu m [1PL know NEG] We didnt know.

(iii) objects of verbs: wo štim ta [3SG insult 2SG] He has insulted you.

(iv) after a preposition, as in (1).          

 

 

(1)                   mun  j         klem  na       na

            3PL     come   speak  with    1PL

            They came and spoke with us.

    

     The suffix pronouns are used after the possessive marker, as in sma t-i [name poss-1sg] my name or waz t-mun [ancestor poss-3pl] their ancestors.

     There is no morphological gender distinction, so adjectives show no agreement: wle sakr a young boy, bina sakr a young girl.

     In Kinubi, the majority of nouns have no morphological plural, the plurality being marked by the plural demonstrative or by a plural word, as in Nbi dol [Nubi PL] the Nubi. However, some words may have a plural suffix, like skol thing vs. sokol-n [thing-PL] things, or bakn place vs. bakan- [place-PL] places; some words may have a plural prefix like nas in nas Mor [PL Moru] the Moru; some have a suppletive form like mra woman vs. nuswn women; and others have a stress shift like bgara cow vs. bagar cows.

     Etymologically, all the numerals are related to the Arabic lexicon and keep the unit-ten order (wy u talatn [one and thirty] thirty-one). However, the speaker may switch to English to express high numbers, like millions or billions. In counting, lf thousand is often followed by wy one, as in (2), which is obviously due to the influence of Swahili (elfu moja mia tisa hamsini na tano).

 

(2)                   lf     wy   tsa           ma u          kmsa     u         kamsn

       thousand          one    nine         hundred    and         five      and     fifty

       1955

 

     The demonstrative pronouns are used as definite markers, e.g. rgi de [man DEF] the man. Indefiniteness may be marked by the omission of the definite marker or by the use of the numeral wy one, as in skol wy [thing one] one thing.

     Noun modifiers — demonstrative/definite marker, adjective, numeral — generally stand after the noun, as in wle kmsa [boy five] five boys, ble de [country DEM] this country, and rujl tinn [man two] two men. However, the word kla each, a Swahili borrowing, precedes the noun (as in Swahili).

     Number agreement is not systematic. On the one hand, we find mun ketir-n [they many-pl] they are numerous, but on the other hand Swahli ya ketr [Swahili cop numerous] Swahili are numerous.

     Generally, the possessor noun or the suffix pronoun follows the possessed noun and is introduced by a genitive marker (t-, rarely bit-), as in kur t-ki [leg poss-2sg] your leg and Nbi ta Mombsa [Nubi poss Mombasa] the Nubi of Mombasa. A possessive construction lacking a marker is also possible but often renders specific expressions or compounds, as in lam gba [animal forest] animal or rutn nbi [language Nubi] Kinubi.

     Unlike Arabic, Kinubi has no special elative form of the adjective; comparison is marked by ftu after the adjective, as in (3).

 

(3)    wo     kebr    ftu           na

         3SG     big       compar     1SG

         He is bigger than me.

 

Ftu may be replaced by zidi min more than:

 

(4)    de        tegl     zidi    min      lger    

         that     heavy  more   than    stone

         It is heavier than a stone.

6.    Verb phrase

 

All the verbs have an invariable stem. There is no derivational morphology, except some compound verbs with s to do.

     The verb has several TAM (tense-aspect-mood) markers, as illustrated in (5), and listed in Table 5.

 

(5)    a.   wo mši              [3SG go]                        he went

         b.   wo kan mši       [3SG IMPF go]             he was going

         c.   wo gi-mši          [3SG IPFV-go]             he goes

         d.   wo bi-mši          [3SG FUT-go]               he will go

     The imperative is formed by the use of the verbal root alone. For some verbs, there is a suppletive form, e.g. tal come (j to come). Likewise, a verb introduced by an auxiliary is non-inflected:

 

(6)    na      zu      klem      me          ta

     Existential sentences are usually formed with f (negated m f), as in

 

(7)    Nbi    f                fi     Knya

         Nubi   EXIST      in    Kenya

         There are Nubi in Kenya.

 

     The main way to render possessive sentences is by using the verb ndisi: na ndisi b [1SG have house] I have a house. Other possibilities exist, such as the use of an existential sentence accompanied by ma with:

 

(8)    Mor     f             ma       rutn            t-mun

         Moru     EXIST   with    language      poss-3pl

         The Moru have their own language.

 

     M is placed before the predicate or the verb as negation: na m rifu [1SG NEG know] I dont know, de m Nbi hes not a Nubi. In Ugandan Kinubi, m or mfi often comes after the predicate: na rifu m [1SG know NEG] I dont know.

7.  Simple sentences

 

The word order at clause level is SVO, as in (9).

 

(9) wo  ro inu  terebya      ta      binia de

     3SG  go   see    education  poss  girl    def

     He will observe the education of the girl.

 

However, topicalization is quite common, as in (10):

 

(10)   mna       t-            na     m          rifu

         meaning  poss-3sg  1SG    NEG      know

         I dont know its meaning.

 

8.  Complex sentences

 

The relative clause is introduced by al:

 

(11)  azl     al         j            min    Yy

         man    REL    come      from  Yei

         the man who came from Yei

 

It may also be introduced by ab:

 

(12)  ajl      ab      ndis   sna    miln   

         man    REL    have    year     many

         the man who has many years; the old man

 

     Generally, the subordinate comlement clause directly follows the verb:

 

(13)  na   fker    lga           de           bi-j                 wduru

         1SG  think   language   DEM      TAM-come     disappear

         I think that this language will disappear.

 

However, in Uganda it may be introduced with the complementizer gl, as in:

 

(14)  ta        gi-klem       gli         sahar

         2SG     TAM-say     say         bewitch.PASS

         You say that he has been bewitched.

 

     Reduplication may be used to emphasize the meaning of a verb, an adjective, or an adverb: yal- dugag-n dugag-n [child-PL small-PL small-PL] very young kids.
